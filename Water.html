Iringan Falls



Iringan Falls
-----

Photo taken April of 2016
by Angel Juarez

Iringan Falls is a serene, horsetail-type waterfall tucked in the lush forests of Pagbilao, Quezon. It cascades from a height of around 15 meters into a crystal-clear, shallow basin surrounded by mossy rocks and dense greenery. Accessible via a scenic trail and river trek, it's a peaceful spot perfect for nature lovers and adventure seekers alike.

ðŸŒŸ Fun Fact: Did you know Iringan Falls got its name from a legendary snake fight? Today, instead of serpents, it's the tourists, vloggers, and painters who are "fighting" for the best shot of this natural masterpiece!

The falls are particularly popular during the summer months when visitors can enjoy the cool, refreshing waters. The area around the falls is also home to various species of birds and small wildlife, making it a perfect spot for nature photography and bird watching. Local guides are available to help visitors navigate the trail safely and share interesting stories about the falls' history and legends.

For those planning to visit, it's recommended to bring proper hiking shoes, water, and snacks. The trail can be slippery during rainy weather, so checking the weather forecast before your trip is advisable. The falls are accessible year-round, but the best time to visit is during the dry season when the water flow is moderate and the trail is less challenging.

More about Iringan Falls



The name Iringan comes from a local legend about two battling snakes, giving the falls a mystical backstory. Once used for road construction and as a rebel hideout, the falls now serve as a vital water source for locals, with plans to supply nearby communities. Tourists, vloggers, and artists like Kuya Pabs flock to the area for its beauty and adventure, from swimming to trekking. While local rules help protect it, threats like pollution and illegal logging still pose challenges to its preservation.

More about Amao Falls



Amao Falls gets its name from Sitio Amao, the nearest area in Silangang Malicboy. Just a kilometer awayâ€”or about a 20-minute trekâ€”from the DENR Region IV-A PASu office, the trail to the falls begins right behind the information center. Before reaching the base of the majestic eight-tier waterfall, visitors must climb a 4-meter-high boulder for the best view. Each tier of the cascade is spaced roughly 8 meters apart. The falls are framed by rich forest vegetation, home to a variety of plant species such as the tibig (Ficus nota), balete (Ficus benjamina), malapapaya, lamio (Dracontomelon dao), tambo, and als-as (Pandanus luzonensis), making it not only a natural wonder but a biodiversity haven.
